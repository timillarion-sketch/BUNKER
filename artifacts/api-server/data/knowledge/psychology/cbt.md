# Knowledge Base: Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

## Overview
Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is a structured, time-limited psychotherapeutic approach that addresses the connections between thoughts, emotions, and behaviors. Developed by Aaron T. Beck in the 1960s, CBT is one of the most empirically supported therapeutic modalities.

## Core Principles

### The Cognitive Model
The fundamental premise of CBT is that distress is not caused by situations themselves but by the interpretation of those situations. The cognitive model follows this sequence:

1. **Situation/Trigger**: An event occurs
2. **Automatic Thought**: A spontaneous, often unconscious interpretation
3. **Emotional Response**: Feelings generated by the thought
4. **Behavioral Response**: Actions driven by the emotion
5. **Physical Response**: Bodily sensations accompanying the emotion

### Cognitive Distortions
Common systematic thinking errors that maintain psychological distress:

- **All-or-Nothing Thinking**: Seeing things in black-and-white categories
- **Catastrophizing**: Expecting the worst possible outcome
- **Mind Reading**: Assuming others are thinking negatively about you
- **Emotional Reasoning**: Assuming feelings reflect reality
- **Overgeneralization**: Drawing broad conclusions from a single event
- **Labeling**: Assigning global negative traits to self or others
- **Mental Filtering**: Focusing exclusively on negative details
- **Disqualifying the Positive**: Discounting positive experiences
- **Should Statements**: Rigid rules about how things ought to be
- **Personalization**: Taking responsibility for external events

## CBT Techniques

### Cognitive Restructuring
Identifying, evaluating, and modifying distorted thoughts:
- Thought records (situation, automatic thought, evidence for/against, alternative thought)
- Socratic questioning (what is the evidence? what is an alternative view?)
- Behavioral experiments to test beliefs
- Examining the utility of a thought (is it helpful?)

### Behavioral Activation
Increasing engagement in rewarding activities to improve mood:
- Activity scheduling
- Graded task assignments
- Behavioral experiments
- Exposure therapy for avoidance behaviors

### Exposure Therapy
Systematic, controlled confrontation with feared stimuli to reduce anxiety:
- Create a fear hierarchy (least to most feared)
- Begin with low-fear items
- Repeated exposure until habituation
- Continue until the most feared item no longer triggers distress

## Treatment Structure
Standard CBT is typically 12-20 sessions:

1. **Assessment** (1-2 sessions): History, case conceptualization, goal setting
2. **Psychoeducation** (1-2 sessions): Teaching the cognitive model
3. **Skill Building** (8-14 sessions): Applying techniques to specific problems
4. **Relapse Prevention** (1-2 sessions): Maintaining gains, planning for setbacks

## Third Wave Approaches
- **ACT (Acceptance and Commitment Therapy)**: Acceptance of internal experiences, commitment to values-based action
- **DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy)**: Emotion regulation, distress tolerance, interpersonal effectiveness
- **MBCT (Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy)**: Mindfulness meditation to prevent depression relapse

## Evidence Base
CBT is effective for: depression, anxiety disorders, OCD, PTSD, eating disorders, substance use disorders, chronic pain, insomnia, and personality disorders. Effect sizes range from moderate to large compared to control conditions.
